While looking at the number of wins in a season is a good place to start, we still can't make a great comparison. In 2016, when Mercedes won 19 of the season's races, there were 21 races. In 2014 and 2015 there were 19 races in each season. If we look at the runs right behind these, McLaren's 1988 run and Ferrari's 2002 and 2004 runs, there were 16, 17, and 18 races in each season respectively.

If we look all the way to the inagural season, 1950, there were only 7 races.

If we want to properly compare the number of wins a constructor had in a season, we have to account for the different number of races in each season.

We can do this by dividing the number of wins in each run by the number of races in that season. For Mercedes's 2016 run it would be:
$$ \frac{wins}{races} = \frac{19}{21} = 0.904761905 $$

Looking at things now, Mercedes's 2016 run is narrowly pushed to second by McLaren's 1988 run.

In 1988, McLaren got the closest to having a perfect season. Drivers Alain Prost and Ayrton Senna won every race in the season except the 1988 Italian Grand Prix.

In the Italian Grand Prix that year, Senna had pole position and Prost was second on the grid, but they did not manage to win. Prost was forced to retire due to engine problems, and Senna, after leading for 49 laps and with only two laps to go, had a collision with a Williams' driver and finished tenth.

In spite of the loss at the Italian Grand Prix, Senna and Prost had a remarkable season, winning 8 and 7 races respectively.

Similarly, Rosberg and Hamilton won 9 and 10 races in their 2016 championship run.

Behind McLaren's 1988 and Mercedes's 2016 runs is Ferrari's 2002 run. In 2002, Michael Schumacher and Rubens Barrichello won 15 of the season's 17 races.

Fourteen of their wins that year were in the robust Ferrari F2002.
